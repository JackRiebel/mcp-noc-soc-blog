What Is MCP?

The Model Context Protocol is an open standard — now governed by the Linux Foundation — that gives AI models a single, universal way to connect to any tool. But to understand why that matters, forget about AI for a second. Think about ordering food.

The Integration Problem — As a Food Order

Before delivery apps, getting dinner meant calling each restaurant separately. Different menus, different phone numbers, different payment methods. Now toggle to see what one universal layer changes.

Hungry People Restaurants Delivery App

That's exactly what MCP does for AI. Right now, every AI model (Claude, GPT, Gemini) needs its own custom integration to every tool it works with — your SIEM, your ticketing system, your network monitor, your Slack. If you have 3 AI models and 6 tools, that's 18 custom integrations to build and maintain. MCP collapses that to 9: each AI connects to MCP once, each tool connects to MCP once, and everything just works. One protocol, universal access — the same leap that delivery apps made for restaurants.

Why NOCs and SOCs Are Drowning

The security operations center was designed for a world with fewer tools, fewer alerts, and more people. That world no longer exists.

Here's what a typical SOC looks like today: an analyst sits in front of 2,992 alerts per day. Not per team — per analyst. Of those, 63% will never even get looked at. The false positive rate runs somewhere between 50% and 83% depending on who you ask. The signal-to-noise ratio is brutal, and it's getting worse.

Meanwhile, the tools designed to help have created their own problem. A small company runs 15-20 security tools. Large enterprises run 76 or more. Every one of those tools has its own console, its own alert format, its own login. Analysts toggle between applications roughly 1,200 times per day, and research shows it takes 9.5 minutes to regain full focus after each switch. That means about 40% of an analyst's productive time evaporates into context switching.

And then there's the staffing crisis. The global cybersecurity workforce gap hit 4.8 million unfilled positions in 2025, growing 19% year over year. Of the analysts who are working, 71% report burnout and 65% say they're thinking about quitting the field entirely. We're asking fewer people to monitor more tools, triage more alerts, and somehow keep organizations safe against increasingly sophisticated threats. The math doesn't work.

The Bottom Line
Your SOC analysts aren't bad at their jobs. They're drowning in tool sprawl and alert noise. The tools were supposed to help — instead, they created a new problem.

What MCP Changes

MCP doesn't replace your tools — it connects them. Here's what that looks like in practice.

Consider a typical security incident. Today, an analyst has to hop through seven different tools: check the SIEM for alerts, validate traffic in the network monitor, inspect endpoints in the EDR console, verify credentials in IAM, create a ticket in the ITSM platform, research context in a knowledge base, and notify the team on Slack. That's 67 minutes and seven context switches — each one breaking focus and adding latency.

With MCP, an AI agent can query all of those tools simultaneously through a single protocol. The analyst gets a correlated summary in about 8 minutes, reviews it, approves the action, and closes the incident. Total time: 12 minutes. That's an 82% reduction.

The financial case is just as stark. AI can theoretically automate 70-90% of common NOC/SOC tasks — log analysis, alert triage, config validation, traffic monitoring — but actual adoption sits between 5% and 25% depending on the task. That gap represents massive unrealized value. IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach report found that organizations using AI and automation extensively saved $1.9 million per breach and resolved incidents 80 days faster. Across industries, AI-assisted operations cut mean time to resolution by roughly a third.

The speed tax is real
Financial services lose $500K per hour of downtime. Healthcare loses $400K. Even utilities lose $100K. Every minute shaved off incident response directly translates to dollars saved — and MCP is how you shave those minutes.

MCP Is Already Everywhere

In just 16 months, MCP went from an Anthropic side project to a Linux Foundation standard adopted by every major AI platform.

The adoption curve has been remarkable. Anthropic created the Python and TypeScript SDKs in September 2024 and launched MCP publicly that November. By February 2025, monthly downloads had crossed 5 million with over 1,000 community-built servers. Then the dominoes fell fast: OpenAI adopted MCP in March (Sam Altman: "People love MCP — excited to add support across products"), Google followed in April with Gemini support, and Microsoft integrated it into Windows 11 and Azure at Build 2025 in May. AWS published open-source MCP servers that November, and by December, Anthropic had donated the protocol to the Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation. As of late 2025, MCP SDK downloads hit 97 million per month.

The enterprise results are already showing up. Block reports a 75% time reduction on daily engineering tasks using an MCP-powered AI agent called "Goose" that connects to GitHub, Jira, Snowflake, and Slack through a single interface. Bloomberg migrated 9,500+ engineers from an internal protocol to MCP — collapsing what used to take days of integration work into minutes. Cisco built a production-ready MCP server for Catalyst Center and ServiceNow with enterprise-grade auth (OpenID Connect + Duo MFA). And AWS has published prescriptive deployment guidance and open-source MCP servers for anyone to use.

Beyond the tech giants, companies like Atlassian, Figma, Asana, and Cloudflare have all built or adopted MCP servers. Yet Census Bureau data shows that even the most AI-forward sectors — publishing (36%) and information services (28%) — have massive room to grow. Most industries sit in the single digits for AI adoption in production. The infrastructure is ready. The adoption hasn't caught up yet.

The Window Is Open — But Not for Long

90% of enterprises haven't started with AI in operations. The analysts say that changes fast.

The forecasts from Gartner and Forrester paint a clear picture of where this is heading. By the end of 2026, Gartner expects 30% of enterprises to be automating more than half their network activities — up from under 10% in 2023. They also predict 40% of enterprise apps will include task-specific AI agents, up from under 5% today. Looking further out, 70% of enterprises will deploy agentic AI for IT operations by 2029. On the vendor side, Forrester projects 30% of enterprise app vendors will ship MCP servers by the end of this year, and tech leaders are tripling their AIOps investments to combat rising technical debt.

But here's the uncomfortable number: Gartner estimates that only 6% of organizations today have the maturity to handle this shift. That means 94% are either not prepared or haven't started. If you're reading this, you have a window — but it's the kind of window that closes quickly once your competitors start moving.

What You Should Do Next

For Network & Security Leaders

  1. Audit your tool sprawl — Count your integrations and identify where analysts are losing time to context switching
  2. Start with one MCP use case — Alert triage, config validation, or incident documentation are high-impact starting points
  3. Talk to your SE — Ask about MCP-ready architectures and what's available for your stack today

For Executives

  1. The talent gap isn't closing — AI-assisted operations is how you compete with lean teams when you can't hire fast enough
  2. 90% of competitors haven't started — Early mover advantage is real, and the window is narrowing as adoption accelerates
  3. Platform consolidation + MCP = force multiplier — Consolidating on fewer platforms and connecting them via MCP compounds the ROI of both strategies

MCP isn't a product you buy — it's an open standard you adopt. The protocol is free. The SDKs are free. The only cost is not starting.
